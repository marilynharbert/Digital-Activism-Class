Threats to photo anonymity

More and more today it seems like no piece of data can be safely anonymized. Anonymous data is disconnected from personal identifiers, such as names and birth dates.[2] To be anonymized would mean that the data could not be reconnected to the person who the data was originally from.[2] A 2019 study from Imperial College London found that 99.9% of Americans could be individually identified with just 15 characteristics from widely available data.[3] Similarly, 2015 MIT study found that 90% of people in an ‘anonymized’ data set of store credit card transactions could be identified.[4] Anonymization techniques that researchers, businesses and others have been using for decades now are being revealed to be inadequate,[2] threatening anonymity in all corners of our lives.

Photos are not exempt from new threats to anonymity. Almost all photos now come with extensive metadata automatically added when the photo is taken.[5] Metadata is “data about data”; meaning the metadata describes the data point in question.[6] This metadata can include fields like: the photographer’s name, the GPS coordinates of where the photo was taken, and the date and time when the photo was taken.[5] And applications like Google Photos, or Apple’s iPhoto can automatically add metadata tagging the faces of people in photos.[5] This metadata can be identified and read by anyone able to access the photo online.[7] While many users may have learned to check the visual context of a photo for content they would find privacy invading to share publicly, many do not realize the amount of information shared in the metadata of photos they share.

However, this privacy threat can easily be combatted if users are aware of it. The anti-surveillance non-profit the Electronic Frontier Foundation writes “Surveillance Self-Defense” guides for different people who are especially vulnerable to surveillance.[8] In their guide for activists attending protests, they advise activists to remove the metadata from any photos they took and are hoping to upload in order to protect their personal information.[9] They suggest the simple method of taking a screenshot of an image, and posting the screenshot to avoid metadata concerns.[9] This method allows people who rely on anonymity online like activists to avoid identification when posting important photos.

But Facebook’s patent, “Associating cameras with users and objects in a social networking system”,[1] presents a new threat to anonymous photos. The patent uses “camera signatures”, unique characteristics of cameras like “faulty pixel positions in the camera and metadata available in files storing the images” to identify other photos taken by the same camera and link them to that user.[1] It also allows Facebook to make connections between different users based off of who is sharing a single camera.[10] In the patent abstract, Facebook’s suggested uses are: “recommending potential connections to a user, recommending events and groups to users, identifying multiple user accounts created by the same user, detecting fraudulent accounts, and determining affinity between users.”[1] The listed uses suggest that Facebook could de-anonymize the accounts but also the links between people posting what were previously anonymous photos.

Before Facebook filed this patent, most people did not realize how much personal information photo metadata reveals about them. However, those who did realize could easily combat this problem, like the EFF suggests by taking a screenshot of the photo before posting. But Facebook’s patent threatens to make those efforts obsolete. If photos can be used to match a photo taken on a personal account with a photo uploaded ‘anonymously’ to a protest page, or if photos used by several different organizations could be linked together to identify one person, then this could put activists and protestors in great danger. But this patent exposes threats beyond just for those who rely on anonymity to stay alive and out of jail.

This patent reveals the issue that those efforts suggested by the EFF were never really enough; metadata has always made our personal information accessible when photos are posted by those who do not know or remember to remove it. We need to problematize the issue of privacy in posting online photos. Additionally, as Raley argues in “Raw Data” Is an Oxymoron, we must understand data collection and the inferences that companies make about us as a result of that data as part of a capitalistic control system.[11] In this modern panoptic capitalistic system, “willingly” give up our data to become seen only as our data by this control system.[11] But the consequences of this control and surveillance are not just a loss of privacy but a system where companies are made powerful by data collection control and manipulate our actions through “dataveillance”.[11] And this system can reinforce or make worse existing inequalities, using digital data to sort and categorize people in real life.[12]

Solutions

I built this website to help with this problem, but it is by no means a perfect solution, just a start. The best solution would likely be if social media companies automatically scrubbed metadata from our photos as we posted them, but their incentives to sell more targeted advertising push them to collect more information about us, not less.[11] The next best solution would be an app which scrubbed all metadata and camera signatures from any photo automatically on one’s computer or phone. This would be best because it would keep the user from having to think about it, before posting a photo, making avoiding surveillance easier and making them more likely to use it. However, because of technical limitations, that is not the project I have created.

Instead, I built a website that I hope achieves two things.

  1. I wanted to give people tools to try to resist some of the surveillance they live under and protect their privacy. Thus there are two tools on this website: one removes metadata from photos, and the other adds some noise to photos in the form of steganogaphic messages in the hopes that will change the camera signature of the photo. The hope is that people can post photos which do not reveal more about them than the contents of the photos itself.
  2. I hope that this can educate people about the information they are sharing unwittingly through sharing photos online, and raise awareness about surveillance in our daily lives.

Methods

Hiding camera signatures with steganography

One common resistance method to digital surveillance is to insert noisy or random data to mask real data collected about digital actions. By using this method, you can attempt to not only hide the real data being collected, but also make reliance on the data not commercially valuable. This method is employed in projects like TrackMeNot, a web browser extension that hides your real search and browsing data from third party trackers by periodically sending noisy packets of data to the tracking companies.[11]

There is also a long history of using a method called "steganography" to conceal information. Steganography is “the practice of hiding secret messages in otherwise non-secret mediums.”[13] Least significant bit steganography involves the practice of hiding images or messages in other images by replacing the last few digits (the least significant bits) of a binary representation of a pixel with a message.[14] This shows that you can randomly change the pixel values of a photo and not significantly change the image.

Digital image steganography uses the last few, least valuable bits used to encode a pixel to hide data. Changing these bits changes the color value of the pixel only very slightly. Thus, you can change the pixels and use the value of those pixels to encode secret messages that can only be decoded by a program reading the values of the last few pixels separately.

Below is an image demonstrating how the Least Significant Bit Steganography works. In the example the bits changed only make up the numbers zero through three, so modifying those bits only changes the pixel value (which can range from 0 to 256 for each color that makes up the pixel) by 0 to 3 numbers.

Metadata Removal

The EFF guide to Surveillance Self Defense explains that removing metadata from an image is possible, and fairly simple.[9] However, many people are unaware of this, and commonly upload photos to the internet with information they did not realize they were including.

Most photo metadata is stored in formats like EXIF, IPTC or XMP.[5] The tool I am using only removes the EXIF metadata tags, which are those added by most phones and digital cameras.[5]

Limitations

Obviously, there are some major limitations to this website. As said before this is not the most convienent method. Additionally, because I have not tested whether a camera signature remains once steganographic messages are hidden, I cannot guarantee that it erases the camera signature. Additionally, because of the limitations of my technical knowledge, I cannot guarentee that the tools made by other developers on github work perfectly, only so far as I can verify them by checking the metadata that tool it registers and the hidden message that the other tool registers.
